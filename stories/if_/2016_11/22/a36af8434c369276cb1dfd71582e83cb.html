Rogers: Jurgen Klinsmann a victim of his own optimism

Martin Rogers , USA TODAY Sports 4:15 p.m. EST November 21, 2016
Skip Ad

USA TODAY Sports' Martin Rogers examines U.S. Soccer's decision to fire head coach Jurgen Klinsmann. USA TODAY Sports

Former USMNT head coach Jurgen Klinsmann.(Photo: Joe Maiorana, USA TODAY Sports)

In the end, Jurgen Klinsmann was a victim of his own optimism as his masterplan for revolutionizing American soccer came crashing down on Monday.

Klinsmann came into the United States head coaching job in 2011 fueled by the promise of a bright new era. It was not all of his own making, both U.S. soccer fans and the national governing body encouraged the belief that snagging the man who had taken Germany to the 2006 World Cup semifinals would lead to a wave of unprecedented success.

Yet Klinsmann did little to temper those expectations, and when he found himself ultimately powerless to get close to them, the net closed in and hastened his demise.

His firing on Monday by U.S. Soccer president Sunil Gulati was, in many ways, an admission that the bold concept of marrying a European approach with traditional American soccer values is no simple task.

USA TODAY

U.S. men's national soccer team coach Jurgen Klinsmann out

To that end, Gulati is expected to go back to what he knows. On Tuesday, he is expected to name former national team coach Bruce Arena as Klinsmann's replacement, a person with knowledge of the decision told USA TODAY Sports. The person requested anonymity because Arena's appointment has not been made official.

Arena is a no-nonsense character whose straightforward ways could be just what the U.S. needs as it bids to get its World Cup qualifying campaign back on track after losing the first two games in spectacular fashion this month.

He inherits a U.S. soccer culture very different to the one he left behind at the end of his first stint in 2006. Attention on the game, and especially the performances of those who represent the country, has never been greater.

FOR THE WIN

Sports world overjoyed with happiness after Jurgen Klinsmann was fired as USMNT coach

Gone are the days when a U.S. head coach gets to operate in relative anonymity in between World Cups. Defeats in the CONCACAF region where the U.S. operates are never going to go down well and a pair of them within the space of a few days, to Mexico and Costa Rica, was enough to spell doom for Klinsmann.

In many ways, bringing in Klinsmann to begin with was an experiment, an expensive throw of the dice to see if the U.S. could fast-track its way, both stylistically and practically, to the top table of world soccer by replicating European methodology.

As he made way for a change of direction of Monday it was clear that the experiment had failed, and that it may not be repeated any time soon.
