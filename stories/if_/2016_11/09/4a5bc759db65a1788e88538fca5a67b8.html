News outlets preach caution on election calls

Roger Yu , USA TODAY 9:27 p.m. EST November 8, 2016

Wolf Blitzer just prior to the Democratic presidential candidate debate at Brooklyn Navy Yard.(Photo: Robert Deutsch)

15 CONNECTTWEETLINKEDIN 5 COMMENTEMAILMORE

No one wants a repeat of the Karl Rove scene this year.

While appearing on Fox News as an analyst on the election night in 2012, the Republican politico threw a hissy fit shortly before midnight ET as the conservative news network projected President Obama winning his second term. This is premature, he said, as Ohio was still being counted. Weve got to be careful about calling things.

In their own diplomatic ways, his colleagues at Foxs decision desk basically told him it was over for Mitt Romney. And the riveting scene of the internal strife now lives on forever on YouTube, with well over a million views.

Caution again is the operative word for the networks and other major news organizations as the hours tick on toward the finality of one of the most contentious presidential races in history. Chastened by the 2000 fiasco -- when several news organizations projected early a Florida victory for Al Gore the networks are emphasizing accuracy over speed.

The race to be the first was more of an issue before 2000, says Joe Lenski, co-founder and executive vice president of Edison Research, whose exit polling data is used by major news organizations. I think the priority now is to be correct. And frankly, theres not 100% trust of the media by a lot of people. We saw that in 2012 with Karl Rove.

Still, impatient voters are addictively seeking the latest updates and projections, however premature. Any they'll get their fix in other places this year as social media, particularly Twitter, and other upstarts finagle their way into the crowded media landscape covering the elections.

    USA TODAY

    When to expect the election to be called, losing candidate to concede

    Votecastr emergedin the current cycle, with its turnout tracker and prognostications throughout the day on Vice.com and Slate.com. Its goal is to publishreal-time projections in battleground states while the polls arestill open, breaking "a decades-long journalistic tradition whereby media outlets obey a self-imposed embargo on voting information under the unproven theory that it might depress turnout on Election Day," according to Slate, which announced its Votecastr partnership earlier this year. Its data are derived fromsample polls taken prior to Election Day and real-time tracking of voter turnout on Tuesday.

    Votecastr's ascendance mirrors one of the trends this year, in which early votes are monitored more closely by journalists, pollsters and Twitter users. Thats gotten more emphasis than it used to, Lenski says.

    But CNN, for one, doesn't see election calling assport, says Sam Feist, CNNs Washington bureau chief. I dont see this as a competition in any way, he says. Thats not part of our conversation. We are not trying to be the first to call. Its not a race between news organizations. We want to be right. We are under no pressure whatsoever (to be first).

    Anthony Salvanto, CBS News elections director, also echoed the accuracy-over-speed approach in the newsroom. My goal is to deliver viewers timely and interesting information that always meets CBS News standards, he says.

    Along with the electoral college, the tradition of media organizations election calls are a remarkably unique American civic tradition. Unlike other nations, the U.S. doesnt have a federal election commission that formally declares results.

    USA TODAY

    Why does the U.S. have an Electoral College?

    U.S. states elections are decentralized with their own infrastructure and set of rules. Certifying results from each state can take hours and days, if not weeks. Without the media actually tabulating votes and telling the public who is ahead or behind or won, wed not really be able to reveal results for the election, perhaps, for weeks after, Feist says.

    In the past, major news organizations that are heavily cited and relied upon had conducted their own exit polls and data crunching. But to make the process more efficient, CNN, the Associated Press, ABC News, CBS News, Fox News and NBC News formed in 2003 a consortium called the National Election Pool. The group now uses exit polling data compiled by Edison Research, which staffs interviewers at about 1,000 voting locations nationwide. Member organizations augment the data with their on-the-scene reporters gathering local precinct data and statistical modeling.

    They all have their own data. And they all present their data differently, Lenski said.

    While announcing results live can seem spontaneous, much preparation goes into production. CNN completed 12 rehearsals with its anchors, data team members and directors modeling various outcomes. Every option is considered, even a third-party candidate winning one of the states, Feist says. Weve been through it so many times. We know what states close when. We know the reporting differences.

    What also makes the current cycle more difficult to project is the prevalence of early voting. In 2000, only about 10% to 15% voted early or absentee, compared to about 40% this year, Lenski said. Some states also have varying requirements for mailed-in ballots.

    To address the change, Edison interviewed about 16,000 people over the phone, Lenski said. Twenty years ago, samples, precinct models and exit polls were sufficient in covering 90% of the votes. Now, if youre doing that, you cover only 60%, he said.
