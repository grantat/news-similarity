The kids aren't all right: Educators grapple with post-election tensions

Anne Godlasky and Greg Toppo , USA TODAY 6:14 p.m. EST November 17, 2016

Harrisonburg High School students, joined by members of the community, cross the street into Court Square to stage a demonstration against President-elect Donald Trump in Harrisonburg, Va., on Nov. 14, 2016.(Photo: Daniel Lin, AP)

More than a week after the election of Donald Trump, educators are struggling to contain a cauldron of tensions bubbling up at many schools in the form of racial incidents and bullying.

Just as the 2016 presidential election emboldened aggressive and sometimes hate-filled displays among adults, teachers say students have shown some of the same behaviors.

"Those kids are terrified,"  says Maureen Costello, director of the Teaching Tolerance project at the Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC). "There's something very, very ugly, and it is erupting at schools, which are places where we just can't afford to let that happen."

In Silverton, Ore., south of Portland, a high school suspended two students on Tuesday who allegedly shouted at Hispanic students, "Pack your bags, you're leaving tomorrow!"

In Silver Spring, Md., near Washington, D.C., vandals late last week wrote “KILL KILL KILL BLACKS” on a bathroom wall at Sligo Creek Elementary School. Maintenance personnel scrubbed the words off.

In Georgia's Gwinnett County, a Muslim high school teacher posted to her Facebook page last Friday that an anonymous student wrote, "Your headscarf isn't allowed anymore. Why don't you tie it around your neck ... and hang yourself with it ... on your neck instead of your head!"

Costello said the SPLC has been tracking the uptick in intolerance at schools. "We've been reporting for six or seven months now a rise in prejudice and bigotry being expressed in school, and teachers having a very, very hard time dealing with it," she said.

Though Trump's campaign has said he disavows hateful rhetoric, his victory on Nov. 8 has been a "B12 shot" to white students who share hateful or intolerant views, Costello said. "They are feeling energized and emboldened."

The incidents put educators into a difficult position, she said. They must remind students that there are "norms of decency" in school, despite what students might be hearing at home or on TV. "We're in uncharted territory."

A few critics say the reports may be overblown. In one case, a student at the University of Louisiana in Lafayette, La., on Nov. 9 said two men, one of them wearing a white “Trump” hat, beat her and ripped off her hijab. She later admitted she'd fabricated the story.

Elsewhere, the fact-checking website Snopes.com pointed out last week that a widely circulated photo depicting a so-called Nov. 9 Ku Klux Klan victory celebration on a North Carolina highway overpass had no KKK affiliation.

Snopes noted, however, that the Loyal White Knights of the Ku Klux Klan had scheduled "a separate victory parade" for Dec. 3 in North Carolina.

In the Georgia incident, teacher Mairah Teli said she shared the note on her Facebook page as a way to “raise awareness about the reality and climate of our community,” WXIA-TV reported. In a statement, the school district said, “A threat against a staff member is a serious matter and we are investigating this incident.”

In Lowell, Ind., about 50 miles southwest of Chicago, one parent said students at a local middle school chanted “Build a wall!” in the cafeteria the day after the election. Throughout their lunch break, white students told Hispanic classmates that they had to sit at the other end of the cafeteria, parent Ashby Gurgon told the education news site The 74 Million. Gurgon said she heard about the incident during “emergency" training with the district superintendent, Debra Howe.

In a districtwide email to parents, Howe wrote that school officials observed “divisiveness and comments made that are making others fearful.”

Administrators in Columbus, Ind., Vice President-elect Mike Pence's hometown, called for respect this week after reports of Hispanic students being taunted with similar chants recently, including on Election Day, the Chicago Tribune reported.

The incidents have included several at a Catholic high school in New York City, where teachers reported to SPLC that:

  • An African-American student was spat on the day after the election while talking about the "Black Lives Matter" movement;
  •  A gay student was told, "You better turn back straight if you want to survive in America."

Reports like these have spurred action by some state officials.

In Pennsylvania, Gov. Tom Wolf said Thursday that a rash of "intolerant, racist incidents" prompted him to create a "safe schools response plan" in which education and law enforcement officials will aid schools in addressing bad behavior.

State Education Secretary Pedro Rivera said schools "must be safe and welcoming for all students, and no student should ever be made to feel that they are not a valued member of the school community.”

New York Gov. Andrew Cuomo on Tuesday  created a statewide hotline to report hate incidents. Cuomo said the increase in reports of discrimination, bias-motivated threats, harassment and violence prompted him to write to the state Education Department requesting that schools hold trainings "to combat intolerance and safeguard New York’s students."

Post-election misbehavior has also claimed a few teachers. In Tuscaloosa, Ala., the school district suspended a high school math teacher after he used a projector to show students an image of Trump mocking President Obama, The Washington Post reported.

The image copied the style and format of Obama’s “Hope” posters: Beneath Trump’s smiling face, the word “Hope” was changed to “Obama, You’re Fired!"

The incidents are befalling colleges as well, authoritees said. Police in New York City this week were trying to find out who scrawled swastikas and profanity on a dorm room door at The New School in lower Manhattan. Police received the report around midnight Saturday; the city's Hate Crimes Task Force was investigating. Student Sam Lichtenstein said she broke down in tears when she discovered anti-Semitic symbols on her door's whiteboard.

We opened up a statewide hotline number to report incidents of bias, because this state celebrates our differences. https://t.co/tFtSGKKu9Z

— Andrew Cuomo (@NYGovCuomo) November 15, 2016

In a statement, New School President David Van Zandt said he'd "take appropriate action to ensure the security and safety" of all of its students.

High school students across the country, many too young to vote, have also been taking to the streets to protest Trump's policies. On Monday, hundreds of students in the Los Angeles Unified School District staged a walkout.

“Although it has been nearly a week since the presidential election, many students remain concerned about the outcome and want their voices to be heard,” the district's Superintendent Michelle King said in a statement at the time. “These are important conversations that need to take place. We want our students to know they are not alone.”

Stephanie Lowe, an art teacher at Ánimo College Prep Academy, a charter high school in Los Angeles’ Watts neighborhood, said students there have displayed a range of emotions, from anger to fear to indifference, since Election Day. Riots devastated Watts in 1965, and presidents, she said, have come and gone, making promises for 50 years.

For many African-American families, she said, “nothing’s changed here since 1965 — and the kids are uniquely aware of that. While this is a really important election, sometimes they’re like, ‘I don’t understand why you’re all worked up. It’s really not going to matter to all of us.’”

But talking to her Latino students, she said, “They’re terrified. They’re scared. I don’t think they’ve ever been scared like that before.”

Jennifer Rodriguez, 18, a senior, said she hasn’t gotten harassed at school — the campus is focused on social justice and enrolls virtually 100% African-American and Latino students. But she has stopped posting on Instagram because “I didn’t really want that abuse from other people. I feel targeted just by posting, ‘Viva Mexico’ or ‘I love Mexico.’”

Rodriguez’s younger sister Patricia, 16, a junior at Ánimo, agreed.

“People do come at me if I post anything about, ‘United We Stand’ or, ‘We’re going to be proud of where we come from.’" she said. "I do get a lot of that thrown at me and it’s sad.”

The pair have helped organize rallies since the election, including an eight-mile march to downtown Los Angeles this week protesting Trump's election. “It’s for him to know: 'You know what? We’re here, and be wise on what you’re going to say, because we’re going to fight,'” Patricia said.

Reflecting on the abuse she and others have experienced on social media, she pleaded with young people: “Be a little bit smarter about what you say.”
