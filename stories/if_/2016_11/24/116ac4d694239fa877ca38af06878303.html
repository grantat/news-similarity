Matt Barkley takes over for Bears, but Jay Cutler could be back

Halas Hall Pass: Jay Cutler and the Bears QB situation

Bears coach John Fox delivered the news with emphasis Wednesday afternoon: Jay Cutler's right shoulder injury is not season-ending.

Repeat: not season-ending.

For a 2-8 team slogging into the stretch run of a demoralizing season, that certainly qualifies as a newsworthy development, perhaps even a hopeful one.

But beyond that prognosis, Fox divulged few specifics regarding Cutler's injury. It's clear the veteran quarterback will miss time. Matt Barkley is preparing to start Sunday at Soldier Field against the Titans. And David Fales, signed off the Ravens practice squad this week, figures to be active as the backup.

But Fox wouldn't say how much time Cutler would miss at the minimum. The Bears coach also wouldn't share when Cutler's shoulder injury surfaced during Sunday's loss to the Giants.

"I couldn't tell you," Fox said. "He finished the game. Everybody saw that."

And when asked whether there was any validity to an NFL Media report that Cutler had torn the labrum in his throwing shoulder, the Bears coach foxtrotted around a direct answer like "Dancing With the Stars" champion Laurie Hernandez.

"There are different levels of injury," Fox said. "Tear is kind of a broad term. (Cutler's shoulder) is sore. There is soreness to it. But there are a lot of things in the shoulder that can affect the quarterback. Some of them can be just chronic, old (issues) — just wear and tear, like pitchers."

In a week that started with widespread worry that Cutler's season could be over, the Bears will now hold out hope for a December return. In the meantime, they'll turn the offense over to Barkley, a fourth-year pro on his third team preparing to make his first NFL start.

Barkley's confidence and poise have always been strengths and the 26-year-old quarterback made it clear after Wednesday's practice that he's pushing to keep his opportunity in perspective.

"I don't want to blow this up out of proportion and look back and say 'Woulda, coulda, shoulda,'" Barkley said. "If I just give my all right now for this one week, and then move on to the next week right after that, that's how I'll best approach this."

To be clear, Barkley takes over a massively depleted offense that will be without receivers Alshon Jeffery and Kevin White and tight end Zach Miller. Pro Bowl right guard Kyle Long is on injured reserve. Pro Bowl left guard Josh Sitton figures to remain out through Sunday with a right ankle sprain. And starting right tackle Bobby Massie was limited Wednesday as he works back from a concussion.

Amid all that attrition, Barkley's performance should be put in context. He also has a short period of preparation to get in sync with his receivers and line, adding to the degree of difficulty.

"It's all about the chemistry we're building in the huddle and the offense," receiver Cameron Meredith said. "The reps in practice will help as much as anything else. And then the extra work after practice that we put in, we'll go in the film room and talk about plays and talk about where he wants us."

Needless to say, the Bears would rather not be in this predicament. Barkley will be the 20th quarterback to start for the franchise since the turn of the century and the third this season. This will also mark the seventh time since 2000 that the Bears have needed at least three starting quarterbacks in a season. They did so most recently in 2011 when Cutler was replaced by Caleb Hanie, who was later supplanted by Josh McCown.

Still, Barkley's college experience as a four-year starter at USC provided plenty of exposure to big games and pressure-packed moments. And that, Fox said, should help equip him for Sunday's test.

"Matt has been in big spots," Fox said. "He won't blink. … It's not going to be too big for him."
