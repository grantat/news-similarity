    Donald Trump, Shinzo Abe, Interpol: Your Friday Briefing

    By CHARLES McDERMIDNOV. 10, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Good morning.

    We’re trying something new for our readers in Asia and Australia: a morning briefing to jump-start your day.

    Here’s what you need to know:

    • The White House transition to a Trump administration opened with an extraordinarily cordial meeting between President Obama and President-elect Donald J. Trump, once bitter political opponents.

    “We discussed a lot of different situations, some wonderful and some difficulties,” Mr. Trump said. “I very much look forward to dealing with the president in the future, including counsel.”

    • Mr. Trump began to assert himself on the world stage. A backer of the “Brexit,” he spoke by phone with Britain’s prime minister, Theresa May, to affirm the “special relationship” between their countries.

    Japan’s prime minister, Shinzo Abe, will meet Mr. Trump in New York next week, seeking to determine whether Mr. Trump will, as promised, scrap the Trans-Pacific Partnership and press Japan and South Korea to pay more for the United States to defend them.

    Beijing faces the unsettling reality that Mr. Trump has a variety of ways to fight Chinese trade practices that he views as unfair.

    • Protests around the United States against Mr. Trump’s election focused partly on fears that pro-white and anti-Muslim extremists could be emboldened.

    Officials at several universities were investigating reports of bias incidents.

    Panic spread through some of the country’s gay and transgender community over the possible loss of Obama-era political gains.

    _____

    • The backers of Mr. Trump’s unorthodox, anti-establishment campaign are now among the candidates for the nation’s top posts, and corporate lobbyists are anticipating allies in the new Republican-dominated government for priorities including repealing financial regulations and spending billions on roads, bridges and other infrastructure.

    Mr. Trump, who has called the notion of human-caused global warming a Chinese hoax, could have an especially powerful impact on climate change policy.

    _____

    • In other news, India deployed thousands of security forces to banks overrun by people desperate to exchange 500 and 1,000 rupee bills (worth roughly $7.50 and $15).

    Prime Minister Narendra Modi abruptly took them out of circulation this week in an anti-corruption bid, giving people just two weeks to exchange the notes for smaller denominations, capped at 4,000 rupees a day, or about $60.

    • There were signs of greater restrictions in Hong Kong in the wake of Beijing’s intervention to block two pro-independence politicians from taking local legislative seats.

    Eight more pro-democracy lawmakers face expulsion over the issue Beijing cited: not properly taking their oaths of office.

    And, citing political concerns, the Hong Kong Center of the Asia Society, an educational group, canceled a screening of a film about the Umbrella Movement, the pro-democracy street protests in 2014.

    • A top Chinese police official has been named the president of Interpol. [Associated Press]

    Back Story

    On this day in 1918, World War I, one of the largest and deadliest wars in history, effectively ended.

    Americans mark the occasion with Veterans Day. The U.S. initially celebrated Armistice Day, but in 1954 the name was changed to include all veterans.

    How do other countries commemorate their World War One troops?

    In Britain and the Commonwealth of Nations, the bright red poppy is an enduring symbol, popularized by “In Flanders Fields,” once called “the perfect war poem” by The Times.

    Canadians celebrate Remembrance Day, which once shared its date with Thanksgiving. To avoid having its veterans overshadowed by food, the government pushed the turkey and stuffing to October.

    While most such events are solemn, Anzac Day in Australia, held in April, has a boisterous side. Revelers pack bars for the rare chance to legally play two-up, above, a gambling coin-flip game popular with soldiers from Australia and New Zealand.

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    Parades and memorials are held in parts of Britain’s former empire, including Kenya, South Africa and areas of India and the Caribbean, even as their World War I contributions are largely overlooked.

    But they are getting new attention. The British Library recently posted a trove of letters by Indian soldiers, including one with a poem.

    Its author writes that his fellow soldiers would “Like tigers, spring upon the prey/And tear the German goat away.”
