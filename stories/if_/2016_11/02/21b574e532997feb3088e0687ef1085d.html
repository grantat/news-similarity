Fathers and sons and World Series dreams

Lifelong Cubs fan Bob Lannon told his son Jack that he would take him to a World Series game at Wrigley Field.

"I've always dreamed of it. At Wrigley, the Series, the Cubs," Lannon told me. "And he wanted to go. So I started looking for tickets."

Just so you know what you're getting into: This is a story written by a White Sox fan about the Cubs and hope and heartbreak and a happy ending. And there's a fellow named Tom Ricketts whom you might know as the owner of the Cubs.

"I wouldn't have believed it either," said Lannon. "But it happened and I can tell you we were stunned, our jawbones were on the ground."

It is a dream of fathers in America, this dream of taking a child to a World Series in your hometown, this handing of baseball down through generations.

Many dream of this but many can't afford it, or work or life gets in the way. I dreamed of it too, once, when my boys were 10 and the White Sox were in the World Series and I had season tickets. And they asked me, hopeful, in their bedroom one night, their room a White Sox shrine. And to my regret, which is still so bitter that it's hard to type this, it didn't happen because I had to work.

And I was thinking about it again the other night, with the Cubs in the series, and scanning my email I spotted a letter from Lannon.

And after I read it, I called him because I wanted to tell you his story.

Lannon, 57, married and in the commercial furniture industry, has three daughters and a son. The kids all play soccer. But his son, Jack, a freshman soccer player at St. Ignatius, loves Cubs baseball.

"He knows all the stats, all the players, and he wanted to go and I wanted to go, so I started looking for tickets," Lannon said.

Most tickets were ridiculously priced, but he thought he'd found what he was looking for on Craigslist. A guy named Vince from the South Side was offering two tickets for $500 apiece. A deal.

"The summary of the tickets was there, in the ad, with the section and seat number, they were upper-deck seats. And Vince pulled me into his web. He said the tickets were in the family, that they couldn't go to Saturday's game, and I fell for it."

Before Saturday's game, a meeting was arranged outside the Merchandise Mart near Lannon's office. The tickets looked legitimate.

"He didn't even open the envelope to count the money," Lannon said. "That's when I knew something was wrong."

At the gate they were told what Lannon already suspected. He'd been scammed. Lannon turned over the fake tickets and Vince's cellphone number and other information from Craigslist to Wrigley staff.

"A young guy in a suit behind the counter said, 'Wait here. I'll see what I can do."

As they waited, what struck him was his son, Jack.

"There was no complaining or whining in him, not one bit," said Lannon. "All he said was, 'That's OK, Dad. It's OK.' He was resolute. And I was so proud of him, the way he handled it."

The guy in the suit returned, and told Lannon he'd found two standing-room-only tickets for face value. They weren't cheap, $250 apiece, but that was cheaper than what he'd paid Vince.

"The Cubs went out of their way for us. And I'm thinking, face value? We'll take them," Lannon said.

He looked at his son. And one look at Jack and Lannon was glad that he took the offer.

Cubs spokesman Julian Green said it was "just by chance" that two standing-room-only tickets were available for Lannon and his son. Green said there is no policy for having tickets available for fans who get scammed. "Anybody can come up and say, 'Oh my God, I got counterfeit tickets and I'm stuck,'" Green said.

Lannon and his son stood toward the first base side of home plate on the mezzanine level. It was packed and loud and it was perfect.

"There were fans from all over, true Cubs fans, and we could see and we were there," said Lannon. "There was a cast of colorful characters, including Frank the plumber. Jack probably heard more colorful profanity than he'd ever heard in his life."

Big Papi, Red Sox great David Ortiz, walked past with an entourage, and other stars and celebrities were there too, with their security guards and hangers-on.

Then an unassuming fellow walked past, no entourage, no glitz, just a Cubs fan alone.

Lannon didn't recognize him. But Jack, the Cubs fan in a Cubs jacket, knew who it was.

"Mr. Ricketts?" said Jack.

Rickets stopped, asked how they liked the game. It was a game the Cubs would lose. But who knew that then?

"Jack started talking to Tom Ricketts and Ricketts reached into his pocket and gave Jack a ball. A game ball, from the World Series game we were watching. It was marked and everything. It's a collector's item now. Ricketts smiled and walked away.

"Our jaws dropped to the ground. We were scammed, we got into the ballpark, and to top it off Tom Ricketts gave my son a game ball."

The next morning, Lannon went to Mass and let Jack sleep in. Mom made pancakes and Jack wouldn't stop holding that ball.

"My wife said he finally jumped up and said, 'I have to put this away. I can't get maple syrup on it!'"

The Cubs lost that one. But as the best seats thinned out, Lannon and Jack sneaked down to the box seats in the eighth inning and another fan made a photograph.

"Think of the life lessons he learned, and the two of us there, at Wrigley, Tom Ricketts and the game ball, and Cubs fans for life," said Lannon.

Fathers and sons. Baseball. World Series. It's what we dream of.
