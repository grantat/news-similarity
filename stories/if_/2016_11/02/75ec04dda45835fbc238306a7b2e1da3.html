Cubs fans bask in warm Cleveland weather, hot start

Nachos, check. Program, check.

Chance to see the Cubs keep their championship hopes alive in the World Series? You bet.

Cubs fans from across the country poured into Progressive Field in balmy downtown Cleveland on Tuesday night, decked head-to-toe in blue and clinging to hope that the North Siders have two road wins in them to snap a legendary title drought.

With the night feeling more like June than the first day of November, Cubs fans settled into the ballpark amid the Indians faithful, another Series starved fan base that brushed aside the invaders from the west, trying to will the home team to a clinching win.

But as the Cubs took an early lead, it was the Cubs fans who temporarily took over the ballpark, shrieking and high-fiving and gyrating with glee.

"Unreal!" one Cubs fan said, as he fist-bumped his friends. "Maybe we'll be staying until Thursday."

A Cubs win in Game 6 forces a winner-take-all Game 7 on Wednesday. Many Cubs fans who made the trek to Ohio said they plan to stay in town, wanting the chance to see the team win a championship with their own eyes.

When Addison Russell connected for a third-inning grand slam, a horde of Cubs fans in the outfield grandstand erupted in wild celebration. One man in a Jake Arrieta jersey dropped to his knees on the patio concourse and extended his arms to the heavens.

Chicago fans behind the Cubs dugout went wild, as did patches of visiting fans from the upper deck to the left field bleachers.

Fans in Cubs blue appeared all over downtown Cleveland earlier in the day, packing the bars and restaurants on East 4th Street, just as many had done a week ago for Games 1 and 2. Fans in Kris Bryant and Ernie Banks jerseys strolled down Ontario Street, one of the city's main arteries, which links the hotel district to the ballpark.

Mike Fischer made a decision while watching Game 5. "If they go to a Game 6, we're going to Cleveland for my old man," he said.

Fischer bought the tickets — $900 apiece — then texted his dad: "Pack your bags."

"I was stunned," said Jim Fischer, who was a baby in 1945 when the Cubs last appeared in the Fall Classic.

The father and son piled in the car Tuesday morning for the three-state trek to the shore of Lake Erie, where they visited the Rock & Roll Hall of Fame after checking into their hotel and grabbing lunch as the Cubs prepared for their game against the Indians a few blocks down the street.

"You don't know if you're going to get another chance," said Mike Fischer, 34, of Crystal Lake, wearing his Cubs cap outside the entrance to the hall of fame, where he enjoyed the exhibit about one of his favorite bands, Phish. "You don't know if it'll be another way-too-many years before they make it back (to the Series)."

Amy and David Weber wore Cubs gear as they stopped to take in the Soldiers' and Sailors' Monument and the fountains at Public Square a few blocks north of the ballpark.

"She talked me into it," David Weber said. "I called in sick to work. We'll assume that'll be fine."

Amy Weber, 36, of Deerfield, said she's been a die-hard Cubs fan ever since childhood, when her dad took her to ballgames. With her favorite team in the World Series, she could not pass up a chance to see them, even if her dad, Bruce Dresdner, couldn't make it. Dresdner is in a nursing home in Highland Park.

"We're at the World Series for him," Amy said.

The atmosphere was more subdued than last week, when there was a Mardi Gras vibe downtown, in outlying city neighborhoods and the Gateway District near Progressive Field. Many Clevelanders turned the day of Game 1 into a citywide holiday, but the lead-up to Game 6 was more reserved, with municipal employees and downtown business workers going about their day and the plaza beyond the left field gate noticeably quieter. Still, it wasn't hard to find someone wearing Indians gear, and many office buildings sported "Go, Tribe!" signs in their windows.

The Cavaliers, the city's NBA team that plays across the plaza from the baseball stadium, moved up the start time of their game an hour to accommodate the World Series game. Sports talk radio crackled with Indians' fans predictions. All but one caller was convinced their team was wrapping up the title tonight.

For the Fischers, the World Series trip was a baseball trip to remember. There was only one thing left on the checklist.

"I don't want any more of this 'Next Year' stuff," said Jim Fischer, 72. "This has got to be the year."
