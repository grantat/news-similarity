Search through 1.2M pounds of trash fails to turn up Jewish prayer boxes

USA Today Network Sarah Taddeo, Rochester (N.Y.) Democrat & Chronicle 7:21 p.m. EST November 17, 2016

New York City Hasidic Jewish residents raised about $25,000 to travel to Perinton to search for religious artifacts that accidentally ended up in the trash. Sarah Taddeo

Jewish community members and High Acres employees sort through containers of trash in search of lost Jewish artifacts.(Photo: Provided, Waste Management)

ROCHESTER, N.Y. — The search for two sets of sacred Jewish prayer boxes in a Perinton landfill was called off on Thursday afternoon.

At least 20 men from a Jewish community near New York City traveled to the area this week to search for the tefillin, which are sacred prayer boxes containing scrolls of Jewish scripture worn on the heads of observant Jewish men during daily prayers. The tefillin accidentally fell into a trash bin at a New York City area synagogue in a moment caught on surveillance video.

After contacting various garbage handling agencies between Rochester and New York City, the community found out that the tefillin were most likely in 32 rail containers of garbage that made their way to High Acres Landfill in Perinton, managed by Waste Management.

Volunteers traveled to Perinton, donned Tyvek suits and searched through 1.2 million pounds of trash before the search was halted.

High Acres workers helped spread refuse from the containers in thin layers on the ground, while the Jewish community members combed through it with rakes, said Nicole Fornof of Waste Management.

But because the tefillin are such precious items to the individual who owns them and to the community as a whole, community members felt strongly about returning to Rochester to find them as soon as possible, said Rabbi David Niederman of the United Jewish Organization of Williamsburg and North Brooklyn, who helped coordinate the search.

"The people and the community are so anxious and heartbroken about this, ‎so they decided to return today to resume the search," Niederman said via email Thursday. Jewish community members have raised about $25,000 so far to pay for hotels, suits and travel to assist the effort.

A man typically obtains his tefillin around the time of bar mitzah, or age 13, when he begins to pray with the sacred items each day, said Niederman. The tefillin may be bought for him or passed down through a family.

The boxes contain verses from four chapters of the Torah, the Jewish holy law that comprises the first five books of the Bible. While the man who lost his tefillin in the garbage would like his original items back, the bigger issue for the Jewish community is the potential for the scripture scrolls to be soiled in any way, said Niederman.

"I’m so grateful that Waste Management is going out of the way to respect the holy scripture and the community trying to search for it," said Niederman.

Calls have been pouring in from churches, hotels and local individuals offering their help, said Jeff Richardson of Waste Management. All callers have been directed to contact Niederman and his team.

The landfill’s been involved with unusual searches like this over the years, said Fornof, with businesses, law enforcement or residents searching for items such as lost rings or criminal evidence.
