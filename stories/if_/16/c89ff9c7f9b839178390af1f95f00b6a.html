Dodgers' Dave Roberts voted NL manager of the year

Jorge L. Ortiz , USA TODAY Sports 7:06 p.m. EST November 15, 2016

Dave Roberts saw his Dodgers club improve after losing ace Clayton Kershaw to a back injury.(Photo: Gary A. Vasquez, USA TODAY Sports)

In his first year running a club at any pro level, Dave Roberts led the Los Angeles Dodgers to one fewer victory than they had the season before.

For that, he was deservedly named the National League’s Manager of the Year on Tuesday, garnering 16 first-place votes and 108 points.

The injury-decimated Dodgers overcame a midseason eight-game deficit in the standings to claim their fourth consecutive NL West title as Roberts’ upbeat attitude and team-first approach spread through a clubhouse previously regarded as fractured.

His steady guidance as a rookie earned him top managerial honors over two veteran skippers, the Chicago Cubs’ Joe Maddon (8 first-place votes, 70 points) and the Washington Nationals’ Dusty Baker (4 first-place votes, 66 points), who had won the award three times each.

On the surface, Roberts merely accomplished the same feat as his predecessor, Don Mattingly, who guided L.A. to the West crown each of the previous three seasons.

However, Roberts faced a much higher degree of difficulty. The Dodgers were beset by an array of injuries to their rotation, losing staff ace Clayton Kershaw for 2½ months and fellow starters Hyun-Jin Ryu, Brandon McCarthy, Alex Wood and Brett Anderson for even longer stretches. Scott Kazmir missed nearly all of September as well, and the Dodgers were forced to constantly improvise as they wound up using 31 pitchers.

Through it all, Roberts remained a picture of positive thinking, refusing to let the team indulge in self-pity. He also found a way to deal with mercurial outfielder Yasiel Puig, who was banished to the minors for a month before being recalled.

“You always want to remember what it’s like to be a player," Roberts told MLB Network, "and becoming a coach and a manager, to have your manager reach out and physically put hands on you every day, I think it sends a message that your care, you’re thinking about them.’’

When the San Francisco Giants faltered after entering the All-Star break with the majors’ best record, the Dodgers pounced and overtook them despite Kershaw’s absence. By the time the three-time Cy Young Award winner returned Sept. 9, Los Angeles was ahead by five games and well on the way to a 91-71 record.

Baker also took over a splintered clubhouse, one that had been further poisoned by the Bryce Harper-Jonathan Papelbon altercation late in the previous season. Under Baker, the once-underachieving Nationals went 95-67 to run away with the NL East.

The Cubs, who wound up winning their first World Series since 1908, enjoyed their best season in more than a century as Maddon led a youth-filled squad to the majors’ best record at 103-58.
