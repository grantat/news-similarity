Gluck: With seventh Cup title, Jimmie Johnson proves greatest of all time

Jeff Gluck , USA TODAY Sports 9:30 p.m. EST November 20, 2016

USA TODAY Sports' Jeff Gluck breaks down Jimmie Johnson's record-tying seventh Sprint Cup championship and all the action from the season finale at Homestead-Miami Speedway. USA TODAY Sports

NASCAR Sprint Cup Series driver Jimmie Johnson (48) celebrates winning the NASCAR Sprint Cup championship after the Ford Ecoboost 400 at Homestead-Miami Speedway.(Photo: Mark J. Rebilas, USA TODAY Sports)

HOMESTEAD, Fla.Jimmie Johnson already was the greatest driver in NASCAR history before Sunday night, when he won his record-tying seventh Cup Series championship.

Five titles in a row during NASCARs most competitive era, plus another three years later, was enough to determine that.

But now that Johnson has No. 7, tied with Richard Petty and the late Dale Earnhardt Sr., theres no disputing hell be a permanent fixture on NASCARs Mt. Rushmore no matter what drivers come along in future generations. Even though it may have taken a few minutes for the feat to sink in for Johnson.

USA TODAY

Jimmie Johnson wins record-tying seventh Sprint Cup championship

There is no way on Earth, Johnson said in disbelief. Just beyond words. Didnt think the race was unfolding for us like we needed to be the champs, but we just kept our heads in the game. Luck came our way and we were able to win the race and win the championship.

Johnson, 41, might not be done yet. Paired with Chad Knaus himself now just one shy of the championship record for crew chiefs with eight titles, a record held by Dale Inman there could be more titles to come.

How high can Johnson set the bar? Maybe he can stand alone at the top with No. 8. Hell certainly have a good shot again next season.

"You think about what it would mean to see Jimmie get eight especially under several different formats," team owner Rick Hendrick said."Im excited to see he and Chad try to break the record now, because were tied. And you cant get to eight until you get to seven."

But whether this is the last one or not, it certainly will be remembered for years. Critics tried to dismiss Johnsons five in a row from 2006-10 as one incredible hot streak, a product of a Chase format that valued late-season excellence over season-long consistency.

Sunday, though, puts a huge dent in that argument.

No one handed Johnson anything this year, as he fought through one of Hendrick Motorsports worst seasons as a company just to make it to the finale. Then his car was found to have illegal modifications before the race, and NASCAR forced him to start in the back of the field.

That might have been a blow for many teams in such a stressful situation, but Johnson and his crew just shook it off. He already was in the top five less than 50 laps into the race though he couldnt maintain his spot.

In fact, Johnson was an afterthought for most of the night. As rivals Kyle Busch, Carl Edwards and Joey Logano raced far ahead of him, Johnson was mired around 10th place. At one point, Knaus was just making changes to the car out of desperation, and it seemed this wouldnt be his year.

But thats the danger about the No. 48 team: You can never count it out. There were so many times during his five straight titles where he overcame all kinds of adversity, and it happened again Sunday.

    Just when it looked grim, a crash between Edwards and Logano with 10 laps to go gave him new life. Another crash sparked an overtime finish, and Johnson was close enough to the front to sniff the lead.

    Once that happened, nobody else had a chance.

    "He is probably the most underrated champion in this sport, to be honest with you," Knaus said."Most people in the situation we were just in would crumble, and he didnt even waver. He knew what he needed to do, he knew what the demands were at that point, and he made it happen. The real spark in this whole thing is Jimmie."

    USA TODAY

    Dale Earnhardt Jr.: Jimmie Johnson is 'the best driver in this era'

    This championship feels different, though, and its not just because of the history-making achievement. The fans at Homestead were craving a Johnson victory, cheering wildly in the final laps when he took the lead.

    This was the year when fans seemingly have started to warm to the idea of Johnson as an icon in the sport. He received more cheers than boos all season, and 58% of fans in an informal Twitter poll before the finale told USA TODAY Sports they were rooting for No. 7.

    USA TODAY

    Gluck: Carl Edwards responds with pure class to NASCAR title loss

    The majority got what it wanted, and the others have no choice but to respect the achievement. Even a change in the Chase format which some said was an attempt to Jimmie-proof it couldnt stop him. In the third year of this elimination-style format, he found the consistency and luck hed been missing to make the final four.

    Earnhardt tied Petty with his seventh title in 1994, so any fan who just reached legal drinking age and younger wasnt alive to see it. But they along with all of us now have witnessed history.

    Perhaps theres more to come.
