Stream These Family-Friendly Classic Holiday Movies

Summon the ghosts of Christmas movies, past and present, for some good cheer. That burning “Yule Log” video keeps visiting friends and family entertained for only so long.

By Monica Castillo
From left, Donna Reed, James Stewart and Karolyn Grimes in “It’s a Wonderful Life.”RKO Radio Pictures

It’s a Wonderful Life

It’s not all good tidings in Bedford Falls, the setting of this Frank Capra classic from 1946. Poor George Bailey (James Stewart) is contemplating suicide after a terrible business deal threatens his family’s well-being just before Christmas. Lucky for him, a benevolent angel (Henry Travers) is there to show him what’s truly important. The film starts light, turns dark, and contains one of the most life-affirming endings of all time.

A scene from “White Christmas.”Paramount Pictures

White Christmas

There’s nothing like a classic Christmas tune to warm up a party. In the 1954 film “White Christmas,” named after Irving Berlin’s yearning song, a song-and-dance duo (Bing Crosby and Danny Kaye) team up with two equally talented sisters (Rosemary Clooney and Vera-Ellen) to save a failing hotel. And if you’re really into the title song, seek out 1942’s “Holiday Inn,” starring Crosby and Fred Astaire.

Peter Billingsley in “A Christmas Story.”Warner Bros.

A Christmas Story

Follow a child’s topsy-turvy midcentury memories as a now-grown Ralphie (Peter Billingsley) remembers the best Christmas present he ever got (a Red Ryder, carbine action, two-hundred-shot range-model air rifle!), the trouble he went to get it, and several misadventures along the way. The 1983 movie’s catchphrase comes from the answer Ralphie gets for his gift request: “You’ll shoot your eye out.”

The Muppet Christmas Carol

Michael Caine plays the grouchy Ebenezer Scrooge in this Muppet-filled adaptation of Charles Dickens’s “A Christmas Carol.” Perhaps it’s the less frightening Muppet ghosts that make this version so kid-friendly. Regardless, Caine’s performance is a delight for all ages.

Edmund Gwenn and Natalie Wood in “Miracle on 34th Street.”Twentieth Century Fox

Miracle on 34th Street

There have been  a few remakes of this 1947 film, but accept no substitutes. A man claiming to be Santa (Edmund Gwenn) is taken to court, and an upstart lawyer (John Payne) tries to prove he’s right. The little girl in the movie is played by a young and precocious Natalie Wood.

From left, June Lockhart, Judy Garland and Lucille Bremer in “Meet Me in St. Louis.”MGM

Meet Me in St. Louis

Break open the MGM vault for this heartfelt 1944 film, in which Judy Garland plays a loving older sister in a family that is told they will be leaving the titular Midwestern city to move to the East Coast. Garland’s rendition of “Have Yourself a Merry Little Christmas” remains one of the most melancholy holiday songs ever.

Will Ferrell and Zooey Deschanel in “Elf.”Alan Markfield/New Line Productions

Elf

This endearing story follows a human raised by elves (Will Ferrell) who ventures from the North Pole to the streets of New York City in search of his real dad (a delightfully grumpy James Caan). As is the case with many Ferrell movies, it’s very quotable. And if you say otherwise, then you are sitting on a throne of lies.

Macaulay Culkin in “Home Alone.”Twentieth Century Fox

Home Alone

By yourself for the holidays? Take some home security tips from Kevin McCallister (Macaulay Culkin), the smart kid in this 1990 John Hughes film, who devises elaborate booby traps to fend off robbers after his parents accidentally leave him behind during their rush to the airport. Take Kevin’s dietary and personal hygiene tips only under advisement.

Andrew Lincoln in “Love, Actually.”Universal Studios

Love, Actually

Love it or hate it, you probably have some opinion about this 2003 holiday movie. Alternating between different characters during the Christmas season, its stories can be sad, sweet and bittersweet, but in the end, there is love. Or, at least some form of it. And a sad dude with handwritten signs and a boom box. An ensemble cast includes Colin Firth, Hugh Grant, Keira Knightley, Liam Neeson, Bill Nighy, Alan Rickman and Emma Thompson.

Chevy Chase and Beverly D’Angelo in “National Lampoon’s Christmas Vacation.”Warner Bros.

National Lampoon’s Christmas Vacation

Everything that can go wrong went wrong in “National Lampoon’s Vacation,” which followed the Griswold clan on its disastrous trip to a theme park. Ditto on their trip to Europe. This third installation in the “Vacation” series offers no break for Clark the patriarch (Chevy Chase) and his beleaguered family. Of course the Christmas tree will be ruined, the turkey will be overcooked, and the in-laws will be most displeased. Best not to speak of what happens to the cat.

Jack Skellington (voiced by Chris Sarandon), left, in “The Nightmare Before Christmas.”Touchstone Pictures

The Nightmare Before Christmas

Halloweenland’s Pumpkin King, Jack Skellington, wants to play Santa Claus (whom he calls Sandy Claws) on Christmas Eve, despite not quite understanding the concept. Based on a story by Tim Burton, this darkly sweet and whimsical stop-motion-animation film by Henry Selick imagines what happens when Halloween’s monsters recreate Christmas with typically Burtonesque flair.
