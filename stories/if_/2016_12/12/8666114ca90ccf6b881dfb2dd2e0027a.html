Frustrated and hamstrung, Dylann Roof's defense team soldiers on

USA Today Network Tonya Maxwell, (Ashveville, N.C.) Citizen-Times 8:26 p.m. EST December 11, 2016

Dylann Roof faces 33 federal charges related to nine deaths in the June 2015 shootings.(Photo: Associated Press)

CHARLESTON, S.C. With his opening statement just finished, in those long moments when a crowded courtroom trickles into the hallway for lunch recess, one of the nations most respected death penalty lawyers turned to his colleague and exhaled heavily.

His lips fluttered and his eyes widened in that universal gesture of exasperation that conveys,I dont know what else I could have done.

What attorney David Bruck tried to do Wednesday in his 24-minute opening address to jurors hearing the case against Dylann Roof is to nudge that panel toward a sentence of life one with no chance of release, he stressed instead of death.

The nine black parishioners shot to death at Emanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church in June 2015 were noble, he said. Then he gently asked jurors to think about what might have driven his client to end those lives.

In response to a damning outline of the case delivered by Assistant U.S. Attorney Jay Richardson, and his promise to prove those facts, Bruck conceded the question of guilt in statements that followed the prosecution.

What you just heard really did happen, he said.

But as the trial marches forward, Bruck and the defense team representing the 22-year-old admitted white supremacist will soon find themselves unable to perform their most critical job.

Roof, in court filings, has said he will represent himself in the sentencing phase, when jurors will consider whether he should live in prison or die by execution.

Roof has entered a not guilty plea, but has also offered to plead guilty if capital punishment is taken off the table. Prosecutors, pursuing convictions on 33 federal charges, have declined.

In a case where the looming question has always been one of life or death, Roofs attorneys are legally hamstrung, unable to submit evidence of mitigating factors that could swing jurists toward a sentence of life.

The defense team has declined comment, but court filings indicate they had planned to argue Roof suffers from a mental defect or similar condition.

That evidence almost always comes by way of witnesses who testify in the sentencing phase, notably, psychologists who have evaluated a client and people like friends and family who can speak to a defendants behavior.

A broken trust is one primary reason capital defendants fire attorneys, though Roof seems engaged with his counselors.

.

Experts look to another reason Roof might want to take control of sentencing: He does not believe he is mentally ill and is blocking his attorneys from presenting evidence that might show otherwise.

That is a classic symptom of people suffering from schizophrenia or bipolar disorder, said Xavier Amador, a psychologist who has offered more than 70 evaluations in capital cases, including Unabomber Theodore Kaczynski and Zacarias Moussaoui, sentenced to life as part of the Sept. 11, 2011 attacks.

In his book, I Am Not Sick I Don't Need Help!he notes half of people living with those illnesses deny they have an ailment, a condition known as anosognosia.

Why do people not believe? The same reasons they have hallucinations and delusions. They have a broken brain. We use our brains to evaluate ourselves, said Amador, who has no connection to the Roof case. When you have schizophrenia, the research is abundantly clear, there is so much scientific consensus, anosognosia is lifelong. Once you have it, you never learn you are ill and these are the defendants who end up firing their attorneys.

Roof, in his own words

You are going to want to understand who on Earth this person was, Bruck, 67, told jurors in his opening statement as part of a delicate attempt to signal the panel should consider the many unknowns about his client.

U.S. District Court Judge Richard Gergel reminded Bruck to limit his statements to guilt, rather than sentencing.

The defense attorney also had noted that Roof does not have a best friend, has few friends at all, in fact, implying that Roofs life is full of lines to be read between.
Jurors on Friday heard writings from a journal Roof authored, one that reflects a four-page manifesto filled with racial hatred, most of it directed toward black people, but also Hispanics and Jews.

Roof had published that manifesto online shortly before the attack at Mother Emanuel, and to read it is to learn of a young man who champions a segregated South, is disappointed that the Ku Klux Klan is not a dominate force and opted to target Charleston, once the countrys most bustling slave port, for its place in black history.

    Amador sees evidence of a psychosis in the writings not readily apparent to the layperson.

    Every paragraph has reference to how afraid he is, Amador said. Being afraid doesnt make someone psychotic, but if they develop fixed-false beliefs, delusions, in the face of great fear or grandiose feelings, then youre dealing with psychosis. His manifesto, frankly could be used as a case history to teach psychiatric residents about identifying delusions.

    Amador notes also the suddenness with which Roof seems to have come to his beliefs, an awakening with the shooting of Trayvon Martin in Florida and with searches regarding black on white crime.

    That kind of rapid grasp of an ideology, along with the flat affect Amador sees in videos are classic signs of a psychosis, Amador said.

    Move for a mistrial

    But without a trained psychologist as a witness, jurors will likely not hear of a mental defect. Prosecutors have said they will put family members of victims and the adult survivors on the stand during sentencing.

    On Friday, the defense team argued they should be allowed to also present evidence on Roofs state of mind, including that he expressed suicidal thoughts on the night of the shootings.

    Gergel told Roofs defense team that he would consider hearing state of mind issues on a case-by-case basis.
    He noted that Roof had not offered an insanity defense and is competent to stand trial.

    At age 21, he wanted to commit suicide, that doesnt rebut any element of the crime, Gergel said of Roof. This is not a back door to insanity light.

    Later, Bruck noted that prosecutors had characterized Roof as a shooter who had no remorse, which can be cited as an aggravating factor favoring the death penalty.

    Gergel stressed he would not let the defense bring in mental health evidence though a backdoor.

    Im not trying to backdoor anything, a frustrated Bruck responded. Im trying to make sure the government does not backdoor anything.
