    GATLINBURG, Tenn. Atop a windingroad that weaves its way up a scorchedmountain abovedowntown Gatlinburg, the city's mayor, Mike Werner, stood behind yellow caution tape on a plot of concrete covered inash and stared out into the Great Smoky Mountains.

    Nearly amonth ago, his family's7,000-square-foot, three-story Craftsman-style home restedon the plot.

    But on this day, Werner stood among its ruins lamenting the aftermath of the late November wildfirethat swept through the tourist town and destroyed his family's home of 21 years.

    To his right, the home's scorched fireplace and stonechimneystood firmly intact.

    But little else remained aside from his wife's burned-out SUV and a bronze pile of charred kitchen appliances.

    "It's only the strength of the Lord that's getting us all through this," said Werner as tow trucks passed behind him on the curvingstreet carting off scorchedcars from neighboring homes. "It's been a daily wake-up of just takingthis one day at a time."

    The Nov. 28inferno killed 14 people, injured at least 180, destroyed or damagedmore than 2,400 structures in Gatlinburg, Pigeon Forge and other parts of Sevier County, and caused more than $500 million in damages.

    Werner, 65, livedwith his wife, Cindy, and five of their seven children in thehouse on Greystone Heights Road just north ofEast Parkway,the main drag that runs through the heart of downtown Gatlinburg. They are amongmore than 2,100 people who lost their homes.

    USA TODAY

    Of the nearly two dozen homes that linethe mountain along theirstreet located directly above Ripley's Aquarium,less than a handful survived the fire.

    All that remained of them this day were chimneys resting on thefoundations of each fire-razed home.

    "We loved this home," said Werner, black glass crunching beneath his feet as he walked into what used to be his living room. "Just to sit outside on our deck and have coffee and stare at the mountains' trees, the colors always changing. It was glorious."

    Another cherished past time he recalled at the home: Cooking in his outdoor kitchen for guests.

    "We entertained here a lot," Werner said. "People would identify this place as a place they could come and relax. But that's all gone now."

    Gatlinburg Mayor Mike Werner stands in the living room, among the ruins of his home of more than 20 years atop Greystone Heights Road on Thursday, Dec. 22, 2016, in downtown Gatlinburg. (Photo: AMY SMOTHERMAN BURGESS/NEWS SENTINEL)

    'That whole mountain is on fire'

    The day the fire hit the city, Werner, his wife and their youngest daughter, 12-year-old Sunni Rae, were inKnoxvilleChristmas shopping.

    "During dinner my phone started blowing up," he recalled.

    "People are being evacuated," one caller said.

    "Don't come back," another warned.

    That same day Warner said his close friend, Davy Thomas, who manageshotels in Sevierville, drove by the bottom of the mountain near the Werner's home and delivered the most gut-wrenching news.

    USA TODAY

    Photographer aims to turn Gatlinburg fire tragedy into art

    "I'm so sorry," Thomas said. "That whole mountain is on fire."

    Werner didn't sleep that night.

    The next day he ignored theheeds to stay away, rushed back to Gatlinburg and drove straight into the burning town.

    Werner, his wife, their son Albert and his mother-in-law then attempted to drive up the mountainto their home.

    "The fire engulfed the whole mountain," he recalled. "We drove as far as we could untilflames kept us from advancing any further. You could smell propane."

    USA TODAY

    "We need to turn back," Cindy Werner told her husband.

    But their son got out and ran up the mountain toward the home.

    "He just had to see," Werner recalled, tears now filling his eyes.

    The family waitedand a short time later, his son ran back to them and delivered the news.

    "It's all gone."

    'I'm going to give you my coat'

    In the days that followed, Werner watchedthe community begin to band together.

    People who never met their neighbors now knew one another on a first-name basis.

    As often as he could, Werner said he would meetwith friends in the community who lost their homesto keep their spirits up.

    "I'd tell them,' he said, cracking a smile, "'Us homeless? We stick together.'"

    City Manager Cindy Ogle and her family also lost their home to the wildfire.

    "We started to make a pact that we wouldn't worry about ourselves until everyone else was taken care of," Werner said.

    Heremembered the outpouring of support from people nationwide, including hisclose friend, Brian Ivey, who traveled from Nashvilleto Gatlinburg the day after the fire.

    "I'm not going to give you the shirt off my back, but I'm going to give you my coat," Werner recalled Ivey telling him, then handing him his yellow coat.

    Just last week, Food City, thanks to donations from its employees, customers and a slew of others, gave$750,000 to help wildfire victims who lost their homes, jobs or a family member.

    Of that, $500,000 goes to theGatlinburg Chamber ofCommerce Foundations Gatlinburg Relief Fund. The rest goes to theDollywood Foundation "My People Fund."

    "Everybody has been just unbelievable as far as their generosities, love and prayers," Werner said.

    'We're going to bounce back'

    Since the fire, the Werner family moved into a house by the riverin Pittman Center just east of town.

    His said his children, including hisoldest son, Michael, who lives in Knoxville, remain optimistic and resilient.

    "Every day since this happened he texts me, 'I love you, dad. Mountain Tough.'"

    Werner's isolatedvisit to his former home that December day was only one of a few sincethe fire.

    "It gets easier every time," he said. "The first time was gut-wrenching."

    He recalled treasures he lost in the blaze, including a framed photo that hung in a hallway of the homeof himself, several city leadersand Sevier County native Dolly Parton. The shot was takenin 1997 when she donated $100,000to open the Dolly Parton Birthing Unit at the LeConte Medical Center in Pigeon Forgeso expectant mothers wouldn't have to drive all the way to Knoxville when their time came.

    KNOXVILLE NEWS SENTINEL

    An open apology to Dolly Parton

    Coincidentally, Werner attended atelethonhosted by the country music singerearlier this month to raise money to benefit her "My People Fund" and bumped into her.

    Gov.Bill Haslam just happened to be standing close by.

    "I handed the governor my phone and said, 'Would you take my picture with Dolly?'"

    The governor kindly obliged.

    The family, he said, plans to rebuild.

    "I'm going to have it demolished. We're going to build back as soon as we can," he said, then lifted his head high. "This was a horrible disaster, but we're not going to let it set us back."

    He said he worriesforothers wholost their homes and prays theyfollow his lead to rebuild.

    "This is the most amazing place to live. This is our home," Werner said, his eyes now bright."People need to know everything is going to be OK and we're going to bounce back."

    "That was yesterday, tomorrow is not here so we're focusing on today and we're hopeful and excited about what the future holds. It's going to be a brandnew day."
