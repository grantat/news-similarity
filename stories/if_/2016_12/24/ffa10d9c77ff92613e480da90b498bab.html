    Their Words to Live By: Artists We Lost in 2016

    By

    Andrew R. Chow

    and

    Lauryn Stallings

    Artists who died this year left behind expansive legacies — some in film or television or music — but all of them in words, whether a special verse, a lyric from a song or a spoken perspective on life.

    Poetry is just the evidence of life. If your life is burning well, poetry is just the ash.

    — LEONARD COHEN
    Musician, born 1934 (Read the obituary.)

    I think there should be laughs in everything. Sometimes, it’s a slammed door, a pie in the face or just a recognition of our frailties.

    — ALAN RICKMAN
    Actor, born 1946 (Read the obituary.)

    Music is spirit, it’s therapy. It makes me feel a certain way, and if played with conviction and soul, the same thing occurs in other people.

    — PRINCE
    Musician, born 1958 (Read the obituary.)

    I believe that television was, and still is, the only medium that can truly reach society’s lowest common denominator and entertain those people who maybe can’t afford a movie or a play. So why not reach them and do it well?

    — GARRY MARSHALL
    Director and writer, born 1934 (Read the obituary.)

    Look up here, I’m in heaven
    I’ve got scars that can’t be seen
    I’ve got drama, can’t be stolen
    Everybody knows me now

    — DAVID BOWIE, “Lazarus,” 2016
    Musician, born 1947 (Read the obituary.)

    They said I was too fat, too black, too short and too old. And look at me now.

    — SHARON JONES
    Soul singer, born 1956 (Read the obituary.)

    This business is like walking through a minefield. They’s why people who envy me look foolish. They don’t see me at the Record Plant, trying all night to get one vocal.

    — GLENN FREY
    Musician, born 1948 (Read the obituary.)

    You taught me precious secrets
    Of the truth withholding nothing
    You came out in front and I was hiding
    But now I’m so much better
    And if my words don’t come together
    Listen to the melody
    Cause my love is in there hiding

    — LEON RUSSELL, “A Song for You,” 1970
    Musician, born 1942 (Read the obituary.)

    When I first met the Beatles in 1962, I didn’t think much of their songs at all. But they learned so quickly how to write a hit. They were like plants in a hothouse.

    — GEORGE MARTIN
    Record producer, born 1926 (Read the obituary.)

    The fog and the smog of news media that logs
    False narratives of Gods that came up against the odds
    We’re not just … rappers with the bars
    It’s kismet that we’re cosmic with the stars

    — PHIFE DAWG, “We the People,” 2016
    Musician, born 1970 (Read the obituary.)

    I have come to believe that the whole world is an enigma, a harmless enigma that is made terrible by our own mad attempt to interpret it as though it had an underlying truth.

    — UMBERTO ECO
    Author, born 1932 (Read the obituary.)

    You have to acknowledge your truth and not let society dictate how you think.

    — FLORENCE HENDERSON
    Actress, born 1934 (Read the obituary.)

    A play is fiction — and fiction is fact distorted into truth.

    — EDWARD ALBEE
    Playwright, born 1928 (Read the obituary.)

    As Willy Wonka [into Mr. Salt’s ear, singing softly]: A little nonsense now and then is relished by the wisest men.

    — GENE WILDER
    Actor, born 1933 (Read the obituary.)

    You learn from what you teach and show people they can achieve beyond what they thought possible for them to do.

    — ZAHA HADID
    Architect, born 1950 (Read the obituary.)

    I compose songs with a lot of simplicity because my school has been the streets and people have been my books.

    — JUAN GABRIEL
    Musician, born 1950 (Read the obituary.)

    Faith is the only way we’re going to make it. None of us are smart enough to do it on our own.

    — MERLE HAGGARD
    Musician, born 1937 (Read the obituary.)

    I once made love for an hour and 15 minutes, but it was the night the clocks are set ahead.

    — GARRY SHANDLING
    Actor, born 1949 (Read the obituary.)

    People generally see what they look for, and hear what they listen for.

    — HARPER LEE, “To Kill a Mockingbird”Author, born 1926 (Read the obituary.)
