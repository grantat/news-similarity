Former Bear Rashaan Salaam found dead at age 42

Photos of Rashaan Salaam, who was the Bears' first-round pick in the 1995 draft.

Dan WiedererContact ReporterChicago Tribune

The news of Rashaan Salaam's death hit those who knew him during his time with the Bears with full force Tuesday afternoon. Ron Turner, Salaam's offensive coordinator for two seasons, couldn't wrap his brain around the reality, recalling the natural ebullience Salaam always seemed to carry himself with.

Robert Green, a backfield mate with the Bears in 1995 and 1996, remembered Salaam's smile, his laugh, his drive and couldn't believe it was all suddenly gone at 42.

"Honestly, I was floored," Green said. "I broke down. Forty-two years old. With the rest of his life ahead of him. This is a tough one to handle. It really is."

Salaam was found dead Monday evening in a park in Boulder, Colo., according to the Boulder County Coroner. An autopsy is being conducted, and according to a statement from the coroner, "the cause and manner of death are pending further investigation."

A source familiar with the case indicated a possible suicide was being investigated. Salaam's mother, Khalada, told USA Today that police said they suspect it's a suicide after finding a note.

"It's so saddening, such a tragedy," former Bears coach Dave Wannstedt said. "I don't ever remember seeing him without a smile on his face. He loved football. He worked. He always had a great attitude."

Salaam was the Bears' first-round draft pick in 1995, selected 21st overall after a brilliant junior season at Colorado. He won the Heisman Trophy that year after becoming just the fourth player in college football history to top 2,000 rushing yards in a season. Salaam rushed for 2,055 yards and 24 touchdowns in his final season with the Buffaloes and enjoyed flashes of success during his first season in Chicago.

He rushed for 1,074 yards — then a franchise rookie record — and scored 10 touchdowns in 1995, earning NFC Rookie of the Year honors.

But that was as good as things would get in the NFL for Salaam, who rushed for only 608 yards and three scores in his final 15 games with the Bears in 1996 and 1997.

He was slowed by a knee injury in '96 then suffered a broken leg and underwent ankle surgery the following year, which led to an injury settlement and his abrupt exit from Chicago. Salaam also struggled often with ball security, fumbling 14 times during his 31 games with the Bears, losing 11 of those.

Turner, though, remembered Salaam as a pleasant young man who was well-liked throughout the organization. As a player, Turner appreciated Salaam's combination of size and speed and the physical nature with which he ran.

"He kind of put pressure on himself at times," Turner said. "He was a perfectionist. But I say that more in a good way, that he demanded a lot of himself. He was such a great team guy."

After leaving Chicago, Salaam's NFL career fizzled during 1999 stops with the Packers and Browns, and he was cut by the Raiders in 2000. He later tried to revive his career with the XFL's Memphis Maniax in 2001.

Salaam was 20 when he was drafted and later admitted he wasn't mature enough to handle everything that came his way. He was also open about his excessive marijuana use during his early days in the NFL.

"When I got hurt, I knew (marijuana) was a problem because it pretty much consumed all my time," Salaam told ESPN in 1999. "I wasn't going to practice or anything like that, so I pretty much spent a lot of my time sitting around and getting high, and that's when I knew I had to let it go. I wasn't outgoing. I was just to myself. All I wanted to do was go home and do what I wanted to do. I wasn't a social person. I was an outcast."

Rumors of a possible suicide had Salaam's former teammates shaken.

"You wonder if there was somebody he could have talked to or something he could have gone to to help him with whatever situation he was going through," former Bears offensive lineman James "Big Cat" Williams said. "It's weird, man, because you hear about all the things the NFL is doing to help the players that are playing now, but players that played in the past, no one thinks about those guys until something like this happens."

Wannstedt recalled the bond Salaam forged as a rookie with Walter Payton, eager to soak in any advice he could from the Bears legendary running back. With Salaam's rookie success, Wannstedt said, there was sincere belief he could become "the next Walter Payton" for the Bears.

When Payton died in 1999, Salaam reflected on his own heartbreak.

"When we lost him it was a sad time in football history," Salaam said in 2001. "My career was going bad, but here's a man at a young age fighting for his life. It put things in perspective."

Now Salaam, too, is gone at a young age.

Green described Salaam as a driven player who was passionate about his job and could be harshly self-critical when he didn't succeed. But it was his personality, his demeanor as "a big happy kid" that will always stick with Green.

"There was a light that he had. He was very self-confident and had that spirit. And even though he was a young pup, he was very grounded in who he was. And he loved to laugh. He had that big, infectious smile that just drew you in. I'm going to try to keep that close to my heart in remembering him."

"Just the fact that the Heisman Trophy is going out again this weekend and then hearing this, it makes me even sadder," Green added. "You wonder what he was going through mentally."
