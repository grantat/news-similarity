Mark Zuckerberg drops his Hawaiian land lawsuits

  • Comment
  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Stumble
  • Email

HONOLULU - Facebook (FB) chief Mark Zuckerberg is dropping lawsuits that sought to buy out Native Hawaiians who own small pieces of land within his sprawling estate on the island of Kauai.

Zuckerberg and his wife, Priscilla Chan, said in a statement to The Garden Island newspaper Friday that they’re ending the cases “to find a better path forward.” They say they “will work together with the community on a new approach.”

The 14 parcels mostly belong to Native Hawaiian families awarded the land during the mid-19th century, when private property was established in Hawaii. 

Many original owners died without wills. Ownership today is split among hundreds of descendants, many of whom are unaware of their shares. 

In 2014, Zuckerberg spent $100 million to buy 700 acres of beachfront land on Kauai’s North Shore.     

The couple filed court cases last month to identify the parcel owners and ask the court to auction the land. The suits filed by Zuckerberg were aimed “at forcing these families to sell their land at a public court auction to the highest bidder,” according to the Honolulu Star-Advertiser.   

The move to get the land through a unique Hawaii law angered locals and prompted a state lawmaker to propose a bill that would force Zuckerberg into mediation before buying real estate on Kauai. 

State Rep. Kaniela Ing said that, in the past, sugar barons in Hawaii employed the same laws Zuckerberg is using to take land from Native Hawaiians, accusing the technology entrepreneur of “completing the theft.”
