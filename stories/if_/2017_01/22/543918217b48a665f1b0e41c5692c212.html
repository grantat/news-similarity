Across the country, on the second day of Donald J. Trump's presidency, demonstrations for women's rights drew massive crowds in cities big and small. Some 670 marches in all 50 states and 32 countries the largest taking place in the nation's capital were planned to send a message to the new president in protest of his agenda.

The crowds were so large in some cities that marching was nearly impossible. In Chicago, organizers halted the march and rallied at Grant Park instead as crowds swelled to 150,000, although thousands still marched. InMontpelier, Vt., state police closed three exits off Interstate 89 due to traffic caused by the march there.

In New York City, crowd estimates were pegged at 200,000as people rallied in and around Dag Hammarskjold Plaza before marching in the streets of Manhattan to Trump Tower. In Boston, there were reports of more than 100,000 people marching in Boston Common to callfor a resistance. On the West Coast, more than 100,000 crowded the streets of Los Angeles to send a similar message.

Hopefully it will lead to greater political engagement," saidRobyn Grier, 34, of Los Angeles, who was taking part in the protest there with her husband.

Here's a look at some of the marches, big and small, that took place across the country Saturday.

Anna Donahue of Ventura (left) and Carol Broderick of Camarillo board a bus in Camarillo, California, for Women's March Los Angeles early Saturday morning. (Photo: USA TODAY Network)

Los Angeles

Thousands of chanting, sign-toting demonstrators filled downtownstarting with a rally in the citys main downtown park, Pershing Square.They carried signs that referenced gender, reproductive rights,immigrationand education.

There were so many people that when the march began, they filled virtually every boulevard leading to City Hall.

I say no to sexism, to being pushed aside, to being lied to, said Leilani Squire of Los Angeles. This whole election just raised all kinds of issues.

Another participant, Gretchen Enders of Los Angeles, carried a sign that said, Be kind always.

She said she came to the protest because this is the time to act. We can show our elected representatives we are a powerful force."

The crowd was estimated at more than 100,000.

A plane pulled a banner around the city's downtown that read "Congratulations President Trump," and as it circled, the crowds roared and broke out into anti-Trump chants.

Ashley McGuire, 25, of Huntington Beachvoted for Barack Obama but stayed home in November. She turned out for the Los Angeles rally to support feminist causes.

I didnt want anything to do with it, she said of the election. I just wasnt interested in the parties running.

    Phoenix

    More than 20,000 people gathered outside the Arizona State Capitol before the Women's March on Phoenix,

    with people laughing, taking selfies with handmade signs and dancing around a drum circle that boomed right by the front door to the state Senate.

    Adam Hall, 41, came with his family from Ahwatukee.

    "This is the first time in a long time I've had hope," he said."It's gotten to a point where you can't just not participate."

    There was a moment of excitement when a helicopter hovered over the expansive crowd and everyone cheered, held up their signs and chanted "love, not hate, makes America great!"

    "I don't see this as anti-Trump but a unifying force for intelligent people who have strong beliefs," said Lynne Avril, a 65-year-old from Phoenix who is the illustrator for the young Amelia Bedelia series."This is a generator of activism, it gets people excited about showing what they believe in. This is the heart of the American people right here."

    Chicago

    In Grant Park, tens of thousands gathered for a rally that grew so large that organizers had to halt the short march that was planned through the city's downtown.

    Unseasonably warm weather appeared to help bolster attendance.

    The Chicago Transit Authority added extra cars to the city's L train service to help transport the mass of people. Still, the trains were packed to the gills.

    Organizers, who projected more than 50,000 demonstrators, said the crowd appeared to have swelled to 150,000. It was held on the same site that former President Barack Obama used for his 2008 election night victory party.

    Juanita Nicholson, 71, of suburban Winnetka, Ill., was attending the first demonstration of her life with her friend Ruth Reagel, 85, who was attending her second protest.

    Finding herself increasingly agitated by Trump's discourse, Nicholson said she was moved to join the crowds in Chicago to send a message. But she said demonstrating is not enough. For her part, she recently started a group in her community to discuss environmental issues and brainstorm ways to affect change.

    "I'm trying not to be negative and be consumed by the things that (Trump says) that make me upset," Nicholson said. "I'm trying remember, and this helps, that we're not alone. I'm trying to focus on doing something positive."

      Austin

      A weary-looking Gail Byler took a break, sitting on the curb of the Texas Capitol driveway with her sign amid an estimated crowd of 35,000 at the Women's March in Austin.

      "I thought we resolved all of this in the '60s and '70s," Byler said Saturday morning, cleaning up the more profane message on her handmade sign. "It got better for a while, but then it got worse again."

      Marchers began floodingthe Capitol lawn hours before the noon starting timeto call attention to women's rights, from equal pay to reproductive choice. But for most, it was one last chance to express their disappointment with the election results.

      "Lock him up!" read one of the countless cardboard signs, an obvious reference to the chant used by the new president's supporters as they expressed their feelings toward Democrat Hillary Clinton.

      Other signs carried messages against racism, against Russian President Vladimir Putin, against a wall along the border and, mostly, against the vulgarlanguage captured in arecording of Trump's conversion with TV personality Billy Bush that surfaced late in the 2016 campaign.

      "Women are not asking for anything more," said Adoneca Fortier, whoseblack T-shirt carried the message "Women Make America Great." "We are asking for equal equal pay and same respect."

      Memphis

      Embracing the spirit of celebration and determination, an estimated 6,000-plus marchers chanted their way through the Memphis Womens March, far exceeding expectations.

      The massive crowd waved to onlookers who recorded the march on cellphones or leaned out apartment windows in a show of support. The march began at the Judge DArmy Bailey Court House, named for a local civil rights icon, and made its way to the National Civil Rights Museum at the Lorraine Motel where Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated in 1968.

      Stand back there and look at that, and its Memphis, said marcher Darcy Locke, 43, of Memphis. How many times do you look at Memphis and its so divided and for once everybody is coming together for something and its nice to see. There are so many different kinds of people here and theyre here for the same reason. Its not just about Trump or womens rights or abortion or anything. Its the right to be.

      Nikki Shaw, 36, of Horn Lake, Miss., saw the march and stopped her car to watch with her mother, Evelyn Shaw, and 11-year-old daughter, Ari Shaw.

      My daughter is biracial. And I wanted her to see that, as blacks and whites, were coming together, Nikki Shaw said.

      I think this is a good thing to show people that we can all come together, said Ari Shaw.

      Montpelier, Vt.

      Sen. Bernie Sanders made a surprise appearance at the massive rally in Vermont's capital city and wasgreeted by a deafening roar.

      He then launched into a fiery speech, calling Trump a fraud.

      "Trump is going to learn they are not going to divide us up!" Sanders shouted. "We are going to create ... a nation based in love and compassion not on hate and bigotry!"

      After finishing his brief speech, Sanders made his way through the crowd to chants of "Bernie! Bernie! Bernie!"

      Capitol complex security chief Tom Cheney said it was the biggest crowd he has ever seen in the capital. He estimated the crowd to be at least 7,000 strong.

      The surge of people pouring into Montpelier led the Vermont State Police to close exits off Interstate 89 leading into the city for a short time.

      Indianapolis

      Women came from throughout central Indiana to support women's rights and protest the presidency of Donald Trump, creating one of the largest crowds seen at the Statehouse in recent memory. Police estimated 4,500-5,000 people attended the rally.

      They chanted "USA, USA," and "they go low, we go high." They sang hymns and songs, such as "We Shall Overcome" and "We'll Be One." They held rainbow gay-pride flags, American flags and Black Lives Matter signs.

      They donned pick hats and brandished brightly colored signs with slogans that included "Make American Sane Again,""I stand with Planned Parenthood and "Elect Women Now."

      Teri Ditslear, the wife of Republican Noblesville Mayor John Ditslear, said she waffled all week about whether to go. She voted for Hillary Clinton, the first time she has voted for a Democrat, because she was concerned about bullying and uncivil remarks by Trump. Ditslear, an abortion-rights minister with Roots of Life Community Church, said her husband supported her decision to attend.

      "I had to be here to be a voice for all women," she said, "to let the world know that women matter and that we are not going away anytime for sure. It just isn't right to keep silent."

      Asheville, N.C.

      Pack Square was the epicenter of the march here, but the crowd of an estimated 7,000 to 10,000 people fanned out into Roger McGuire Green, pressed up against surrounding buildings and spilled into the sidewalk and streets.

      Mayor Esther Manheimer addressed the crowd, a sign language interpreter at her side.

      "People have asked me, 'Why are the women marching?' " Manheimer said. "And I tell them, this past year is the first year of my lifetime and in all of your lifetimes that women did not take a step forward in their march to equality.

      "The road to equality lays long before us, and on that road we have challenges like income equality, violence against women, protecting a women's right to choose and of course one day, a woman in the White House."

        Carrying signs while wearing rain boots and ponchos, hundreds braved wet weather and thunderstorms as they marched through downtown in a show of solidarity with women's marches in other cities around the world.

        Naples, Fla.

        The mood here was light on a sunny and warm dayas an estimated crowd of 2,500 to 3,000 ranging from toddlers being wheeled by their moms to an 89-year-old Korean War veteran.Selfies, or "us-ies" as one of the speakers called it, were a popular part of the walk.One resident sat at his curb, handing out fresh bottles of San Pellegrino; motorists inthe rapidly shrinking traffic lane honked and waved.

        "l'm here because of fear. Fear of what the future will bring and hope for greater understanding for everybody," said Mary Day, 68, who was participating in her first march. "I've always been a wallflower even though I was a flower child. I just wanted to come today."

        Norine Hemping, 70, of Naples, said she had "dearly wanted, in my older years, to see a good woman in the White House." She was carrying a peace flag she and her husband had found in a Key West shop but said she had a laundry list of reasons she had joined this march.

        "I feel like I'm back in the '60s and we have to do this over again. I'm marching for public education, for all voter rights, for immigration rights, for our Muslim brothers and sisters, for our country not to misuse the military and to stop the abuse of religion for political purposes," she said.

        Senaca Falls, N.Y.

        At least 5,000 people marched down the main thoroughfare of Seneca Falls, home to the first Convention on Womens Rights in 1848, organized by known suffragists like Elizabeth Cady Stanton.

        These are not issues that are new, but they have a renewed importance, said Betty M. Bayer, of Geneva, one of the events organizers.

        The crowd eventually took to the street, walking about five blocks from the towns Women Rights National Historical Park to First Presbyterian Church of Seneca Falls, chanting about democracy and singing This Land is Your Land.

        In some way, theres unity thats been spurred on by all this dissent, said Aleta Brown of Rochester. Its a necessary struggle.
