    The Playlist: 21 Songs From 2016 That Nearly Got Away

    By

    Jon Pareles

    ,

    Jon Caramanica

    and

    Nate Chinen

    Every Friday, pop critics for The New York Times weigh in on the week’s most notable new songs and videos — and anything else that strikes them as intriguing. You can listen to this playlist on Spotify here. Like this Playlist? Let us know at theplaylist@nytimes.com, and sign up for our Louder newsletter (coming soon!) here.

    Noname, ‘Yesterday’

    Fatimah Warner, who records as Noname, starts her mixtape “Telefone” with “Yesterday,” a song about her grandmother’s funeral and a lot more: celebrity, memories, insubordination, self-doubt, nostalgia, gratitude. Noname is from Chicago and has appeared on tracks by Chance the Rapper; her delivery is all her own. She’s sometimes rhythmic, sometimes spoken-word conversational, sometimes sung and always calmly assured. The smooth backup track of “Yesterday” hints at Stevie Wonder and gospel-R&B;, with twinkly electric piano and a hip-hop backbeat, but her vocal comes at it from every angle, cannily destabilizing it to make herself heard. JON PARELES

    Roberto Fonseca, ‘Family’

    The Cuban pianist Roberto Fonseca's 2016 album, "ABUC," put vintage Cuban styles through assorted time warps. "Family," with its genre billed as "Doo-wop/Electro Aragón,” is the nuttiest of them. Mr. Fonseca takes frisky solos on organ, and the tune layers the kind of rock that might have shown up in a mid-1960s TV theme with a raucous New Orleans horn section, vocals from members of the Cuban institution Orquesta Aragón a bridge that down-shifts into a slow cha-cha and a street-parade coda. It ends up somewhere between Santana and Buena Vista Social Club, and a long way from tradition. A live version on YouTube is fun, too. J.P.

    Bayonne, ‘Appeals’

    Everything loops and tinkles in “Appeals” by Roger Sellers, a.k.a. Bayonne, a one-man band — not only in the studio but also live — who assembles dizzying pop songs that all segue together on his album, “Primitives.” He’s clearly a disciple of pioneering tape-loop Minimalists like Terry Riley and Steve Reich using far more modern gear. And he’s probably well aware of Animal Collective; he exults in percussive, hypnotic consonance. Like much of the album, “Appeals” is full of sounds that plink and ping — pianos and bell tones, high and low — and vocals stacked into dazed mantras over rock drums and guitars. “I couldn’t imagine how it made you feel/nothing is real and nothing appeals,” he sings. Is it the isolation of the self-sufficient music geek? J.P.

    Charly Black, ‘Gyal You a Party Animal’

    A prototypical dancehall reggae slow-burner. First released in 2014, this song, a peppy celebration of a girl who can “flip it like a Flipagram,” became one of this year’s most uplifting and cheerful anthems. It’s uncommonly smooth — the beat is the Jambe-An riddim (produced by DJ Kurt Riley) — and found wide acceptance, whether on the New York hip-hop station Hot 97, or in the world of reggaeton, where artists including Maluma, Daddy Yankee and Farruko made remixes. JON CARAMANICA

    DJ Luke Nasty, ‘Might Be’

    Anderson.Paak released “Might Be” in 2015 — it was gentle, lush, a careful whisper. It received a little notice, but not much. This year, DJ Luke Nasty did something between a cover and a remix of it, remaking the song as a steroidal come-on, amping up the drums and the bawdiness. It went to No. 78 on the Billboard Hot 100. Glean from that what you will. J.C.

    DJ Q, ‘Sonic’

    A blistering yet bubbly grime instrumental built out of Sonic the Hedgehog sound effects. Clever and inevitable and a refreshing shock. J.C.

    William Michael Morgan, ‘I Know Who He Is’

    In the emergent field of gentleman country — an antidote to the bros of the last few years — William Michael Morgan stands tall, with good posture and a modest mien. This song is one of the best from his sturdy, reflective debut album, “Vinyl.” It’s a throwback to the temperate wisdom of midcareer George Strait, and a needed breather after all the pickup trucks break down and all the lakeside parties get broken up. J.C.

    Gallant, ‘Bourbon’

    A sterling modern quiet-storm anthem with mystic lyrics: “I'm a headless horseman on quilted sand dunes/With my neck wide open, I pray for refuge.” This is the most bracing song on Gallant’s beautiful debut album “Ology,” a collection of spacious R&B; rooted in 1980s-inflected experimentation. J.C.

    Elysia Crampton feat. Chino Amobi, ‘Children of Hell’

    The most provocative and unstable songs on Elysia Crampton’s “Elysia Crampton Presents: Demon City” are with Chino Amobi, especially “Children of Hell,” a jumble of gothic doom, soul whimsy, and industrial crunch that sounds like it’s at war with itself, with several rhythmic ideas shouting over each other to be heard. J.C.

    Jim Black Trio, ‘Song H’

    The drummer and composer Jim Black is known for hair-trigger volatility, but in his smart acoustic trio, with Elias Stemeseder on piano and Thomas Morgan on bass, he also finds cause for deep sonority and rustling reflection. Last spring the band rolled into Le Poisson Rouge to provide a tantalizing preview of its third album, “The Constant,” which was released in August. The album deserved more attention than it has received: it appeared on hardly any year-end lists, which seems in retrospect like an oversight. For a picture of the album in brief, hear “Song H,” which compresses a lot into a five-minute track: a prickly prepared-piano line, a mournful near-chorale, a resonant bass solo. And a groove that dissolves and hardens again. NATE CHINEN

    Wolfgang Muthspiel Quintet, ‘Father and Sun’

    Wolfgang Muthspiel, an Austrian guitarist with a graceful and lyrical style, put together a legitimate all-star team for his new album, “Rising Grace.” Along with the unsurpassable rhythm team of Larry Grenadier on bass and Brian Blade on drums, which he has enlisted before, he brought in the trumpeter Ambrose Akinmusire and the pianist Brad Mehldau. The album errs a bit on the side of shadowy beauty — it’s an ECM release, and seems to take that fact seriously — but there’s a spark in this band that should be sustained. Judge for yourself with this video, a live performance of one of the album’s best tracks, “Father and Sun.” N.

    Catherine Russell, ‘You’ve Got the Right Key But the Wrong Keyhole’

    Jazz Age revivalism has many shining stewards, but no one more to-the-manner-born than Catherine Russell. That’s an actual fact: Ms. Russell’s parents, Luis Russell and Carline Ray, were notable figures in jazz during the 1930s and ’40s. Her own career has hardly followed a straight line — she has toured with David Bowie, and provided background vocals to dozens of other pop acts — but she can give the impression of having sung throwback standards, without distraction, for all her life. “Harlem on My Mind” is her latest album, and it transcends any novelty airs even on a song like “You’ve Got the Right Key But the Wrong Keyhole,” about which no more need be said. N.C.

    Justin Carter, ‘Know It All’

    If you know Justin Carter, you probably know him as a D.J., one-half of the Brooklyn dance label and party host Mister Saturday Night. “The Leaves Fall,” a forthcoming solo album, reveals another side of Mr. Carter, as a singer-songwriter enamored of chiming British psych-folk. “Know It All” is the album’s lead single, released digitally and as a seven-inch single. It features pizzicato cello and a subtle growl of organ, along with lyrics that revel in comeuppance. Catch up with the song, which was released a few months ago, and you’ll also have a leg up on the album, due out on Jan. 27. N.C.

    The Cookers, ‘The Call of the Wild and Peaceful Heart’

    Hard bop has no greater present-day vessel than the Cookers, a collective of seasoned travelers in the style, like the pianist George Cables and the drummer Billy Hart. The band, which is closing in on a decade of work, released another strong and searching album this year, “The Call of the Wild and Peaceful Heart.” The title track was composed by Billy Harper, who originally released it just over 40 years ago. But there’s nothing dated about its sound here, especially as Mr. Harper tears into a tenor saxophone solo, around the five-minute mark. N.C.

    And for good measure, seven more thumpers

    That Nation, “Whole Notha”

    The Americanos feat. Ty Dolla Sign, Lil Yachty, Nicky Jam amd French Montana, “In My Foreign”

    Nick Grant feat. WatchTheDuck, “Get Up”

    King T.R.A.P. feat. Maxo Kream, “Dry Snitch”

    Kronic Young Fresh, “Bad Bish Challenge”

    Famous Dex feat. Chino & Rich the Kid, “U Late”

    Jidenna, “Little Bit More”

    J.C.
