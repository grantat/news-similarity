13 things you probably never knew about Martin Luther King's college years

By Holly Epstein Ojalvo 5:16 pm EST January 13, 2017

95 shares
SHARE
TWEET
SMS
SEND
EMAIL

Have you ever wondered what Martin Luther King Jr. was like in college? Here’s a peek at the college years of the young man who went on to become the most important and influential civil rights leader in American history.

1. King started college when he was only 15

US clergyman, civil rights leader and future Nobel Peace Prize winner Martin Luther King, Jr. speaking in London on Sept. 21, 1964. (Photo: AFP/Getty Images)

As King skipped two grades in high school, he started at Morehouse College in Atlanta — the only college on the planet for black men — at age 15, without formally graduating from high school. That was in 1944, according to Biography.com.

2. He was a legacy at Morehouse

King’s grandfather and father had also gone to Morehouse. Later, so did his brother as well as his two sons, Martin Luther King III and Dexter Scott King.

3. He got his bachelor’s degree in sociology

It’s the study of “how the social world works,” according to the Morehouse sociology department.

4. A class he took junior year set him on his path

According to Biography.com, he wasn’t all that interested in following in his father’s footsteps into the ministry until he took a Bible class as a junior. He was ordained and then first became an assistant pastor as a student, in 1948, according to Washington State University.

5. After college he went to a seminary

Crozer Theological Seminary in Chester, Pennsylvania, where he became both class valedictorian and elected student body president. There, he got a second bachelor’s degree, in divinity.

6. He wasn’t the best undergrad student

His grades were only so-so, according to Stanford’s King Encyclopedia. And though he later became famous as a Baptist minister, in college he drank beer and played pool — considered by many at the time to be a vice.

7. But he was actively involved in extracurriculars

According to the Stanford King Encyclopedia, at Morehouse King was “a member of the debate team, student council, glee club, and minister’s union,” as well as the local NAACP chapter. He also played on a YMCA basketball team and twice won second place in a speech competition.

8. King started speaking out for civil rights in college

In the summer between his sophomore and junior years, he published a letter to the editor in the Atlanta Constitution about what black Americans wanted:

“We want and are entitled to the basic rights and opportunities of American citizens: The right to earn a living at work for which we are fitted by training and ability; equal opportunities in education, health, recreation, and similar public services; the right to vote; equality before the law; some of the same courtesy and good manners that we ourselves bring to all human relations.”

9. He wrote an article in the student newspaper

As an 18-year-old senior, King wrote a piece in the Maroon Tiger, the student paper, about the value of education: “We must remember that intelligence is not enough. Intelligence plus character — that is the goal of true education.” He concluded, “If we are not careful, our colleges will produce a group of close-minded, unscientific, illogical propagandists, consumed with immoral acts. Be careful, ‘brethren!’ Be careful, teachers!”

10. He seriously dated a white woman during seminary school

As Salon reported, while he was at Crozer seminary, he “fell in love with a German cafeteria employee named Betty,” and his friends, knowing his father wouldn’t approve of his interracial relationship, tried to talk him into breaking it off. (This was the 1940s; the Supreme Court didn’t rule in favor of interracial marriage until 1967.)

11. He got his Ph.D. at Boston University

He was accepted at Yale and other schools, but he chose BU for his doctorate, which he earned at age 25.

12. His doctorate was in ‘systematic theology’

And History.com says his wrote his dissertation on “A Comparison of the Conceptions of God in the Thinking of Paul Tillich and Henry Nelson Wieman.”

13. He got his first job as a pastor while still working on his dissertation

Embed from Getty Images

In 1954, he was named pastor of the Dexter Avenue Baptist Church in Montgomery, Alabama, before he finished his degree. He also got married before he finished his Ph.D.
