Acting U.S. attorney general directs Justice Dept. not to defend Trump travel ban

  • Comment
  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Stumble
  • Email

Acting attorney general Sally Yates announced Monday evening that the Department of Justice will not defend President Trump’s executive order banning new arrivals from seven Muslim-majority countries.

Yates, a career official and Obama appointee, said in a statement that she is “not convinced” the order is “lawful” and that the Justice Department would not defend it in court “until I become convinced that it is appropriate to do so.”

Yates, who is technically the deputy attorney general, is in charge of the Justice Department until Sen. Jeff Sessions, Mr. Trump’s pick for the attorney general post, is confirmed.

Sessions’ confirmation vote is scheduled for Tuesday, Jan. 31.

“If he is confirmed we can expect that he will reverse this guidance,” said CBS News justice reporter Paula Reid.

In the meantime, Mr. Trump could fire Yates. 

“As acting attorney general she serves at the pleasure of the president and he could certainly fire her,” Reid said.

“He has power to do it but we’ll see whether or not he chooses to use that in the final hours before he gets his pick for attorney general,” Reid said.

The executive order banning new arrivals and refugees from the Muslim countries had already been challenged in court a number of times, and has sparked widespread protests in major cities. On Saturday, the Justice Department defended the order before a court in Brooklyn.

Yates’ decision signifies a major about-face by the department even if it is symbolic. Sessions is expected to be confirmed by the Senate on Tuesday, and will then be able to reverse Yates’ decision and defend the order.
