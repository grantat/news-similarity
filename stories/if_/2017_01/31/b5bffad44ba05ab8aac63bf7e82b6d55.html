    Who will Trump add to the Supreme Court?

    President Trump will fill at least one vacant seat on the Supreme Court during his term. Here are his possible picks to replace Justice Antonin Scalia and how they could fit in with the justices on the bench. Trump is scheduled to announce his nominee at 8 p.m. Tuesday.

    Three front-runners

    Trump has said he will choose from a list of 21 possibilities released during the campaign, and sources involved in the process say a handful of federal appeals court judges have emerged at the top.

    [ Trump close to naming nominee for Supreme Court]

    William Pryor, 54

    Judge on U.S. Court of Appeals for the 11th Circuit in Alabama

    Pryor is a protege of Trump’s pick for attorney general, Sen. Jeff Sessions (R-Ala.). His past comments on gay rights and stalwart support of the death penalty have made him the nominee that liberal groups say they would most fiercely oppose. Several conservative groups have also objected to a decision he joined that upheld the right of a transgender woman to sue over being fired.

    Profile: Perhaps the most polarizing Supreme Court justice possibility

    Law school alma mater: Tulane University

    Career: Pryor served as a private attorney after clerking on the U.S. Court of Appeals. He also served as attorney general of Alabama.

    Key opinions

    Neal Gorsuch, 49

    Judge on the U.S. Court of Appeals for the 10th Circuit in Colorado

    Gorsuch, 49, would not bring the outsider credentials represented by others on Trump’s list — graduated high school from Georgetown Prep and his mother was head of the Environmental Protection Agency. He is seen as a reliable conservative with a reputation for clear and lucid writing.

    Profile: Neil Gorsuch naturally equipped for his spot on Trump’s Supreme Court shortlist

    Law school alma mater: Harvard

    Career: Clerk for Justices Byron White and Kennedy; deputy assistant attorney general at the Justice Department

    Key opinions

    Thomas Hardiman, 51

    Judge on U.S. Court of Appeals for the 3rd Circuit in Pennsylvania

    Hardiman was the first person in his family to go to college, and he financed his law degree by driving a taxi. Conservatives praise his record on gun rights.

    Profile: Judge Thomas Hardiman has taken an unorthodox road to the federal bench

    Law school alma mater: Georgetown University

    Career: Hardiman serves on the 3rd Circuit with Trump’s sister Maryanne Trump Barry.

    Key opinions

    The rest of Trump’s choices

    During his campaign, Trump released a list of 21 possible nominees with help from two conservative groups — The Federalist Society and The Heritage Foundation. The remaining 18 people on the list includes one U.S. senator, eight federal judges and nine state Supreme Court justices, though a nominee without experience enforcing federal laws is rarely confirmed. Eight of the potential nominees worked as clerks for justices at the Supreme Court. The list also includes two brothers — Thomas Lee and Sen. Mike Lee, from Utah.

    How they compare to Scalia

    A group of researchers attempted to measure the likelihood that each potential Trump nominee would be the most Scalia-like of the group. Because the measure is based on their judicial decisions, the researchers only included nominees with experience as judges. They based their conclusions on three things: how the judge interprets the constitution; how often they cite Scalia’s writings about how to think about interpreting the law; and how often the judge writes a separate dissenting opinion, something Scalia did quite often. The researchers also considered the pre-Supreme Court judicial careers of Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Alito for comparison.

    Time spent considering Supreme Court nominees

    In the past 115 years, the average time for Senate confirmation/rejection has been 41 days, but the process has required an average of 73 days since the Nixon administration. The Senate refused to schedule hearings for Merrick Garland, President Obama’s nominee for Scalia’s seat. His nomination, which automatically ended when the 115th Congress was sworn in on Jan. 3, lasted a record 293 days.
